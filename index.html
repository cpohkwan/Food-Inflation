From Lunchflation Woes to Global Food Inflation Pains

As I grieve over the 25-cent hike in my daily caffeine fix, I wonder how people in some parts of the world are coping with triple-digit hyperinflation.

By Cheong Poh Kwan

For years, I could grab this cup of coffee at my school’s canteen for S$0.55 (US$ 0.39). Bringing my own mug will save me another 10 cents.

I'm paying close to 30% more for my daily cup of coffee.

That changed a few weeks ago when the price was jacked up to $0.70. No more discount for eco-conscious efforts either.

While facing lunchflation as a salaried staff, I also find myself having to raise the selling price of my homemade soup paste from S$8.8, my favorite number, to $9.50, my husband’s favorite number. But the marginal increase is hardly enough to cover the 20% hike in our packaging cost and another 20% hike in our delivery cost thanks to soaring oil and gas prices.

I've increased the selling price of my basilicious soup paste marginally.

Singapore’s food inflation rate stood at 4.5% in May and may double in the months ahead, according to Nomura Holdings’ projection. The rate last peaked at 9.3% in June 2008, according to historical data in Koyfin.

Given that Singapore imports more than 90% of its food, the number could have been far worse if not for more active intervention by the central bank, which has allowed the currency to strengthen against those of its major trading partners.

Singapore’s food inflation rate remains lower than most of its neighbors in Southeast Asia, many of whom are net exporters of oil and agricultural produce. Among the 10 countries in the bloc, Brunei and Vietnam have the most benign inflation readings. Myanmar hasn’t published any data in 2022. Its last recorded rate in Dec 2021 was 12.4%.

Compared to the rest of the world, Southeast Asia in fact hasn’t fared too badly. Excluding Myanmar, the region’s mean food inflation rate is 5.2%, far lower than the global average of 17.4%.

The average is skewed to the right by outliers like Lebanon (364%), Zimbabwe (225%), Venezuela (155%), which have been grappling with hyperinflation for years following their currency collapse, largely a result of political corruption and monetary mismanagement. Turkey, Sri Lanka and Argentina are also struggling with runaway inflation.

The global median is 10.1% - exactly the same rate recorded in the US in May 2021.

A small number of countries manage to buck the trend and keep food inflation below 2.5%. China is one of them. It’s largely self-sufficient in food and its zero-Covid strategy has also weakened domestic demands.

Switzerland’s food inflation remains low at 1.1% as the strong Swiss franc is able to keep imports cheap. Many of its peers in Europe, on the other hand, are fighting to rein in double-digit inflation after the Russia-Ukraine war disrupted the global commodities supply chain.

When countries are grouped by World Bank’s income classification, the mean food inflation rate is the highest for those in the upper-middle income category, and lowest for high-income countries. The median rate is similarly high for upper-middle and low-income countries.

According to risk consultancy Verisk Maplecroft, three quarters of the countries that are now facing high risk of inflation-triggered social unrest are middle-income countries.
